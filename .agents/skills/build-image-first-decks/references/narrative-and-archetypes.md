# Narrative and slide archetypes

Use this reference when turning source material into a slide sequence. The objective is not to showcase every possible slide type; it is to move one audience through one argument.

## Narrative rules

1. Define the audience's starting belief and the desired ending belief.
2. Give each slide one job that can be stated in one sentence.
3. Make the headline carry the claim; do not rely on body copy to reveal why the slide exists.
4. Use evidence immediately after the claim it supports.
5. Alternate compression and release: dense proof should be followed by a simple synthesis or visual breather.
6. Change visual silhouette when the rhetorical move changes, not merely to create variety.
7. Remove repeated conclusions, generic agenda slides, and decorative detours.
8. Close with a specific next action, decision, or remembered idea.

## Common arcs

### Pitch or proposal

1. Promise
2. Stakes or problem
3. Why the status quo fails
4. New mechanism or approach
5. Evidence
6. Differentiation
7. Plan or offer
8. Ask

### Launch or keynote

1. Provocative opening
2. Change in the world
3. Human tension
4. Product reveal
5. Experience or mechanism
6. Proof
7. Future state
8. Invitation

### Report or strategy

1. Executive conclusion
2. What changed
3. Evidence
4. Cause
5. Implication
6. Options
7. Recommendation
8. Decision required

### Teaching or explainer

1. Question
2. Mental model
3. Mechanism
4. Example
5. Counterexample
6. Application
7. Summary

## Archetype entry conditions

Use an archetype only when its condition is satisfied.

| Archetype | Use when | Avoid when |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Cover | Establish the promise and visual world | Adding agenda, disclaimers, or several claims |
| Statement | One short claim deserves full attention | The idea needs several qualifications |
| Big number | One sourced figure changes the audience's view | The number is invented, weak, or one of many peers |
| Evidence | A claim needs concrete proof | The slide is only decorative data texture |
| Comparison | Two real options share comparable criteria | Creating a strawman opponent |
| Process | Ordered stages matter to understanding | The items are merely a list |
| Timeline | Time or sequence is the actual argument | Dates are approximate decoration |
| Quote | A real attributable voice changes credibility or emotion | Using fabricated praise or generic inspiration |
| Portrait/story | A real person or user experience is central | Using anonymous stock-style people as filler |
| Diagram | Spatial or causal structure is simpler than prose | Exact technical topology cannot be safely generated as pixels |
| Visual breather | The audience needs synthesis or emotional reset | It repeats the previous claim without advancing the story |
| CTA | The final action or decision is specific | Ending with a generic thank-you slide |

## Copy budgets

These are heuristics, not quotas. Shorter is usually safer for image-rendered text.

- Cover: title plus one short support line.
- Statement: one sentence.
- Big number: one figure, one label, optional source marker.
- Evidence: one claim plus up to three concise evidence labels.
- Comparison: two sides, up to three matched points.
- Process: three to five steps with short labels.
- Quote: one short excerpt plus attribution.
- CTA: one action and the minimum contact or next-step detail.

If accurate copy does not fit the budget, split the slide. Do not compress essential nuance into illegible microcopy.

## Fact discipline

- Maintain exact spelling for names, products, organizations, metrics, units, dates, and quotes.
- Record the source for claim-bearing slides in the manifest.
- Label estimates and scenarios as such.
- Do not infer a logo, customer relationship, endorsement, or market position from visual familiarity.
- When the visual is metaphorical, keep the factual claim literal and modest.
